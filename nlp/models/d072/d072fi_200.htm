[1] Leonard Bernstein--pianist, composer, conductor, teacher, TV personality, liberal, and bon vivant--died at 72 on October 14, just four days after announcing his retirement from conducting
[2] He had been in failing health since last spring, suffering from a variety of lung diseases, and cut short a Japanese tour in July
[3] He was buried on October 16
[4] "Lenny", the first American conductor to attain world fame, was the well-educated son of Russian Jewish immigrants
[5] A member of the first class at Tanglewood, he achieved instant stardom at 25 as an emergency fill-in to conduct the New York Philharmonic on nation-wide radio
[6] He conducted this orchestra over 2000 times
[7] He composed both classical and popular music and introduced thousands to classical music on television
[8] He made more than 200 classical recordings, many now being examined for re-release
[9] Plaudits have come from everywhere and the New York Philharmonic presented four all- Bernstein nights of classical selections
[10] In Los Angles, three orchestras and two radio stations honored him
[11] On November 14, his career was commemorated at Carnegie Hall and Broadway said goodbye on December 13
[12] He had savored the celebrations of his 70th birthday, which featured all-star fundraising concerts at the Tanglewood Music Center and Carnegie Hall.
